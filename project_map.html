Geographies of Climate Change in the United States

By ZACH LEVITT

Increasing temperatures. Rising sea levels. More frequent natural disasters. Economic upheaval. Worsening social inequality.

Climate change poses grave threats to the world yet it can often feel like an invisible and distant threat. This is especially true for many in the United States, where the crisis is highly politicized and it is easy to feel guarded from the worst effects. So what would it take for Americans to understand the ways in which climate change could fundamentally alter life and worsen existing inequalities?

With this challenging question as its premise, this project seeks to make climate change in America more visible and personalized by visualizing its geography. Utilizing data from a recent 2017 study of estimated climate change damages for every county in the United States in 2100 (Hsiang et al. 2017), I examined the expected change for several economic and social indicators, including county GDP, agricultural yields, mortality rates, and crime rates. The projections reflect estimated changes in these indicators between 2080 and 2099 due to climate change.

Due to the nature of these data the raw changes are less meaningful than the relative geography of impact. By focusing on how each county's risk compares to the rest of country, this project aims to contribute to the development of a geography of climate change in the United States.

Perhaps the most devastating impact of climate change on human populations will be increasing mortality rates. The South, especially the rural counties of the Southeast, are expected to suffer the greatest increases due to rising temperatures while Northern counties may experience lower rates due to more temperate climates.

These patterns are represented on the map by showing the relative severity of a certain county's estimated change compared to the rest of the country. For example, the change in mortality rate expected in red counties is worse than at least 80% of all counties in the country.

This map displays change in the number of deaths per 100,000 people, meaning the changes are scaled to the population of a given county. However, the map would suggest that these changes will be felt equally in rural counties like Loving County, Texas (about 100 people) and urban counties like Los Angeles County (over 9 million people).

Therefore, it is helpful to visualize the geography of climate change while overlaying the existing population geography of the United States.
