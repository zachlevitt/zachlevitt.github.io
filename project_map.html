Geographies of Climate Change in the United States

By ZACH LEVITT

Increasing temperatures. Sea level rise. Frequent natural disasters. Economic upheaval. Worsening social inequality.

Climate change poses grave threats to the world yet often appears as an invisible and distant threat. This is especially true for much of the United States, where the crisis is highly politicized and it is easy to feel guarded from the worst effects.

Therefore, this project is guided by two primary questions. First, how will climate change impact the United States? And second, what is the geography of climate change effects look like? By visualizing and evaluating the estimated impacts, this mapping tool hopes to motivate people to recognize how climate change could alter life in the United States by 2100.

Utilizing data from a recent 2017 study of estimated climate change damages for every county in the United States (Hsiang et al. 2017), this project examines a range of economic and social indicators, including county GDP, agricultural yields, mortality rates, and crime rates. The data reflect estimated changes in these indicators between 2080 and 2099.

Due to the nature of these projections the raw changes are less meaningful than relative impact. By focusing on how each county's risk compares to the rest of country, this project aims to contribute to the development of a geography of climate change in the United States.

Visualizing the geography of climate change depends on the existing population geography of the United States. While counties are a helpful unit of analysis for this study, their populations vary significantly.

For example, how do you compare places like Los Angeles County (over 9 million people) and Loving County, Texas (just over 100 people). This project visualizes population values using brightness, meaning highly populated counties will be displayed with brighter colors while less populaed counties will appear dimmer. One of the most extreme effects of climate change will be an uneven geography of mortality due to rising temperatures. The South, especially the rural counties of the Southeast, are expected to suffer the greatest increases in mortality due to rising temperatures while Northern counties may benefit from more temperate climates.

The white to red gradient represents the relative severity of a certain county's estimated change compared to the rest of the country. For example, the change in mortality rate expected in red counties is worse than at least 80% of all counties in the country.
