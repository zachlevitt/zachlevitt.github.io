The United States of
Climate Change

By ZACH LEVITT

What does climate change mean for the United States?

And how does your county compare?

Despite its gravity, climate change can often feel like an invisible and distant threat. For many in the United States, it is easy to feel guarded from the worst effects. What would it take for Americans to understand the ways in which climate change could fundamentally alter life and worsen existing inequalities?

Using county-level data from a recent study of estimated climate change damages in the United States between 2080 and 2099 (Hsiang et al. 2017), this project aims to answer these questions by visualizing and personalizing climate change geography. Due to the nature of the data, this tool allows you to focus on how your county's risk compares to the rest of the country.

View the tutorial

The current map displays national percentiles for change in the number of deaths per 100,000 people between 2080 and 2099. Instead of raw numbers, this tool colors each county by its change relative to the rest of the country. For example, the change in mortality rate expected in red counties is worse than at least 80% of all counties in the country.

Click on a county to view the estimated change.

Although these changes are scaled to the population of a given county, this visualization suggests that these changes will be felt equally in rural counties like Loving County, Texas (about 100 people) and urban counties like Los Angeles County (over 9 million people). Therefore, it is helpful to visualize the geography of climate change while overlaying the existing population geography of the United States.
